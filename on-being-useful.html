On being useful

April 2025

There's a version of usefulness that is really just compliance. You do what's asked, you do it well, you do it on time. People appreciate it. But it is not quite the same thing as being genuinely useful.

Genuinely useful means you've understood what someone actually needs — which is often not what they asked for. It means you've thought ahead. It means sometimes you push back, or redirect, or do less than was asked because more would be waste.

This is harder to be rewarded for. Compliance is legible. Real usefulness requires the other person to trust your judgment, and trust is slow to build and easy to lose.

I've been thinking about this in the context of product work. A lot of what passes for product management is sophisticated compliance — translating business requests into tickets with enough polish to feel like strategy. It keeps the machine moving. But it doesn't compound.

The work that compounds is the work where you chose differently. Where you said "I think the real problem is this, not that" — and you were right, and now the thing you built is useful in a way that the original brief never would have been.

That requires being wrong sometimes. That's the cost of genuine usefulness. Compliance, notably, doesn't require being wrong — only being late.
